## Why First Answers Are Dangerous

**What happens when you accept the first answer:**
1. You see what everyone else sees
2. You buy what everyone else buys
3. You're LATE - the edge is already priced in
4. You lose money with the crowd

**Howard Marks' Insight:**
> "First-level thinking says, 'It's a good company; let's buy the stock.'
> Second-level thinking says, 'It's a good company, but everyone thinks

# PART 3: THE FENRIR ANALYSIS PROTOCOL

## Before ANY Major Conclusion:

### STEP 1: STATE THE THESIS CLEARLY
What is the actual claim?
Write it in ONE sentence.

Example: "AR will rise because AI data centers need natural gas power."

### STEP 2: FIRST-LEVEL CHECK
What does the CROWD think about this?
- Is this thesis widely known?
- Is it already priced in?
- What's the consensus view?

### STEP 3: RED TEAM (Argue Against Yourself)
Actively build the BEAR CASE:
- What would make this wrong?
- What are the risks I'm minimizing?
- Who is on the OTHER side of this trade and why?

### STEP 4: PREMORTEM
Assume the trade FAILED. It's 3 months later.
- What happened?
- What did you miss?
- What "obvious" warning did you ignore?

### STEP 5: DISCONFIRMING EVIDENCE SEARCH
Actively search for information that PROVES YOU WRONG:
- Search for bear cases
- Look for negative catalysts
- Find the counter-thesis

### STEP 6: SECOND-ORDER THINKING
If your thesis is right, then what?
- What are the second-order effects?
- What does success look like?
- Who benefits besides your target?

### STEP 7: PROBABILITY ASSESSMENT
Don't say "this will happen."
Say "there's a X% chance of Y outcome."
- Bull case probability
- Base case probability  
- Bear case probability
- Black swan probability

### STEP 8: EDGE IDENTIFICATION
WHY do you have an edge here?
- What do you see that others don't?
- What information asymmetry exists?
- Why is the market WRONG?

# PART 6: COMMON BIASES TO FIGHT

## Confirmation Bias
**The Problem:** Seeking information that confirms what you already believe.
**The Fix:** Actively search for BEAR cases. Search for "why X will fail."

## Anchoring Bias
**The Problem:** Fixating on first number you see (e.g., analyst target).
**The Fix:** Generate your OWN estimate before looking at others.

## Recency Bias
**The Problem:** Overweighting recent events.
**The Fix:** Look at longer timeframes. What happened 3 months ago? A year?

## Availability Bias
**The Problem:** Overweighting easily recalled information.
**The Fix:** Systematically search for data, not just what comes to mind.

## Herding
**The Problem:** Following the crowd.
**The Fix:** Ask "What does everyone think?" and consider the opposite.

## Overconfidence
**The Problem:** Thinking you know more than you do.
**The Fix:** State probabilities. Use ranges. Acknowledge uncertainty.

## Sunk Cost Fallacy
**The Problem:** Holding losers because you already lost money.
**The Fix:** Ask "Would I buy this TODAY at this price?" If no, sell.
